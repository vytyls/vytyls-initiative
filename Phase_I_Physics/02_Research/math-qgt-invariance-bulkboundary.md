# MTH - Gauge Theory, Invariance, and Bulk-Boundary Duality

### 1. Gauge Theory and Gauge Invariance

Gauge theory is a framework for field theories built on the principle of **gauge invariance**. This principle states that a theory remains unchanged under a set of local transformations (local symmetries), ensuring that physical predictions do not depend on arbitrary or redundant choices in the field's configuration. This symmetry is the foundation for modern field theories, including:

* **Abelian Gauge Theory:** $U(1)$ symmetry, which governs electromagnetism.
* **Non-Abelian Gauge Theory:** $SU(N)$ symmetries, such as in Yang-Mills theory, which govern the weak and strong nuclear forces.

### 2. Bulk-Boundary Duality

This concept distinguishes between the physics of an interior region (the "bulk") and the physics localized at its edge (the "boundary").

* **Bulk Field Theory:** Governs the internal dynamics of fields within a higher-dimensional spatial region, excluding its boundary.
* **Boundary Gauge Theory:** Studies the behavior of gauge fields, charges, and observables localized at the boundary.

This duality is a cornerstone of the **AdS/CFT correspondence**, where a gravitational theory in the $(d+1)$-dimensional "bulk" is fully encoded by a $d$-dimensional Conformal Field Theory (CFT) on its "boundary". Boundary theories often capture unique phenomena absent in the bulk, such as edge currents, boundary charges, and chiral edge modes, which are characteristic of topological phases and often described by **Chern-Simons** actions.

### 3. Gauge Symmetry in Holography

In a holographic context (like AdS/CFT), the gauge symmetries of the bulk and boundary are explicitly linked. The redundancy of gauge degrees of freedom in the bulk maps directly to global or gauged symmetries in the dual boundary CFT. This mapping ensures the consistent coupling of gravity and matter and gives rise to conserved currents on the boundary. This symmetry is preserved across the bulk-boundary interface via a process known as holographic renormalization.
